QR codes are everywhere — restaurant menus, business cards, event tickets, and product packaging. If you need to create one, this guide covers everything you need to know.

## What Is a QR Code?

QR stands for "Quick Response." A QR code is a two-dimensional barcode that can store data horizontally and vertically, allowing it to hold much more information than a traditional barcode. When scanned with a smartphone camera, the encoded data is instantly decoded and acted upon.

QR codes were invented in 1994 by Denso Wave, a Japanese automotive company, to track vehicle parts during manufacturing. Today they are used in marketing, payments, authentication, and much more.

## What Can You Encode in a QR Code?

QR codes are extremely versatile. Common uses include:

- **URLs** — Link to a website, landing page, or social media profile
- **Plain text** — Display a message, instructions, or any text content
- **Email addresses** — Open a pre-filled email compose window
- **Phone numbers** — Prompt the user to call a number
- **SMS messages** — Pre-fill a text message
- **WiFi credentials** — Let guests connect to your network by scanning
- **vCards** — Share contact information that saves directly to a phone
- **App store links** — Direct users to download an app

## QR Code Best Practices

### Size Matters

A QR code that is too small will not scan reliably. Follow these guidelines:

- **Business cards:** At least 0.75 x 0.75 inches (2 x 2 cm)
- **Posters and flyers:** At least 1.5 x 1.5 inches (4 x 4 cm)
- **Billboards:** Proportionally large enough to scan from the viewing distance

### Contrast Is Key

QR codes need high contrast to scan properly. Dark modules on a light background work best. Avoid light-colored codes on white backgrounds or dark codes on dark backgrounds.

### Include a Call to Action

A QR code by itself may not motivate people to scan. Add a short text like "Scan to visit our website" or "Scan for WiFi" next to the code.

### Test Before Printing

Always test your QR code with multiple devices before mass printing. Camera quality, lighting conditions, and scanning apps can all affect readability.

### Error Correction Levels

QR codes have built-in error correction that allows them to be read even if partially damaged or obscured:

- **Level L** — Recovers up to 7% of data
- **Level M** — Recovers up to 15% of data
- **Level Q** — Recovers up to 25% of data
- **Level H** — Recovers up to 30% of data

Higher error correction means the QR code can be partially covered (like placing a logo in the center) but also means a denser pattern.

## Static vs Dynamic QR Codes

**Static QR codes** encode data directly into the pattern. Once generated, the content cannot be changed. They are free and work without any third-party service.

**Dynamic QR codes** encode a short redirect URL. The destination can be changed after printing, and you can track scan analytics. They require a service provider and usually cost money.

For most personal and small business uses, static QR codes are the better choice — they are free, have no dependencies, and work forever.
